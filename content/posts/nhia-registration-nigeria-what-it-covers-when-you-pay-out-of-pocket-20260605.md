If you ask many Nigerians why they delay going to hospital, one answer comes up again and again: cost.

That is exactly why health insurance keeps coming up in search, conversations, and hospital front desks. People want to know whether the National Health Insurance Authority, or NHIA, can actually reduce what they pay, how to join, and what happens when a clinic says a service is "not covered."

This is a timely question. In November 2025, Nigeria's Federal Ministry of Health and Social Welfare said health insurance coverage had risen from 3 percent in 2019 to [17 percent, representing about 37.4 million Nigerians](https://health.gov.ng/66th-nch-fg-cross-river-govt-reaffirm-commitment-to-universal-health-coverage-expand-financial-protection-equitable-access-for-nigerians/). In May 2026, the same ministry said coverage had expanded by [over 33 percent](https://health.gov.ng/president-tinubu-launches-critical-infrastructure-projects-in-health-and-145-tricycle-ambulances-to-enhance-emergency-medical-response/). That is progress, but it also means most Nigerians are still navigating healthcare costs with incomplete protection.

The pressure is real. The WHO Africa Health Observatory profile for Nigeria says [out-of-pocket spending accounts for more than 75 percent of total health expenditure](https://ahop.aho.afro.who.int/download/health-financing-nigeria-health-system-and-services-profile/). A May 2026 Commonwealth Fund profile also notes that out-of-pocket payments still account for [about 76 percent of total health spending](https://www.commonwealthfund.org/sites/default/files/2026-04/2026_Country-Profiles_Nigeria.pdf).

So here is the practical guide.

## What is NHIA?

NHIA is the National Health Insurance Authority. According to the agency, it was established under the [National Health Insurance Authority Act signed on 19 May 2022](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/), replacing the old NHIS framework.

The NHIA says its role is to ensure [financial access to healthcare for all Nigerians and legal residents](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/). In plain language, the goal is to reduce the amount families pay directly when they need care and to move more Nigerians into prepaid, organized coverage.

## How do you register for NHIA in Nigeria?

Nigeria's Federal Ministry of Health and Social Welfare says you can [visit the NHIA website or your nearest NHIA office to enroll](https://health.gov.ng/faqs/). The ministry says you will need:

- a valid ID
- biometric data
- a choice of registered healthcare provider

There is no single route for everyone. Your path depends on your work and family situation.

### If you are a salary earner

The NHIA FAQ says the Formal Sector programme is for [public and organized private sector employees](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/). The same FAQ says employers pay 10 percent and employees pay 5 percent of salary under that structure.

### If you are self-employed, run a small business, or want family coverage

The NHIA's Private Health Plan page says these plans are available for [self-employed people, staff of small, medium or large corporations, families, and community group members](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/service/health-insurance/). The page says enrollment happens through approved HMOs and that users can choose from the list of accredited HMOs.

### If you are asking about state schemes or vulnerable support

The Ministry FAQ also notes that [some categories are covered under the Basic Health Care Provision Fund](https://health.gov.ng/faqs/). In practice, many Nigerians may also interact with state health insurance schemes alongside federal NHIA structures, especially if they are in the informal sector or enrolling through state-supported programmes.

## What does NHIA usually cover?

This is where people need realistic expectations.

The NHIA FAQ describes the advantage of its programmes as [cashless treatment and comprehensive coverage](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/). The NHIA Private Health Plan page says members can access:

- doctors' visits
- hospital stays
- emergency care
- medications

It also says the plan can cover a spouse and children under 18, with the option to add more family members for an extra fee.

There are also government-funded health services outside what many people think of as ordinary insurance cards. The Federal Ministry of Health and Social Welfare says [certain maternal and child health services, vaccinations, tuberculosis treatment, HIV treatment, and some emergency services](https://health.gov.ng/faqs/) are available free under public programmes or subsidized schemes.

That said, do not assume every bill disappears once you enroll.

## What NHIA does not mean

NHIA is not the same as "every service at every hospital is free."

The NHIA FAQ says:

- you can only select [NHIA-accredited facilities](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/)
- NHIA does [not cover all ailments](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/)
- there are [exclusions and partial exclusions](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/)
- kidney dialysis is covered, but [limited to six sessions yearly](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/)

That is the part many patients miss. Insurance helps, but it works within a benefit package, a provider network, and specific programme rules.

## When might you still pay out of pocket?

Even if you are enrolled, you may still face direct payment in situations like these:

### 1. You go to a facility outside the accredited network

The NHIA says enrollees should use [NHIA-accredited facilities](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/). If you choose a provider outside that list, your coverage may not apply.

### 2. The service falls under an exclusion or partial exclusion

The NHIA explicitly says not all ailments are covered and some items are only partially covered. If a test, procedure, or medicine is outside the package, you may have to pay the difference yourself.

### 3. You need additional family members covered

Standard family coverage has limits. The NHIA FAQ says contributor, spouse, and [four biological children under 18](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/) are covered in the standard setup, while the Private Health Plan page says more dependants can be added for an extra fee.

### 4. The hospital or pharmacy has a supply problem

The NHIA FAQ says that if prescribed drugs are out of stock, [providers must source the prescribed drugs](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/). In the real world, this is one area where patient frustration often starts, because delays, substitutions, or unclear communication can still push families toward extra spending.

## Questions Nigerians should ask before enrolling

Before choosing a plan, HMO, or provider, ask these practical questions:

- Is this hospital or clinic NHIA-accredited?
- Which consultations, tests, admissions, and medicines are covered?
- What services are excluded or only partly covered?
- How many dependants can I register?
- What is the complaint process if I am denied a covered service?

That last question matters. The NHIA says complaints can be lodged with [NHIA offices, HMOs, or the NHIA call center](https://www.nhia.gov.ng/faq/).

## What hospitals and clinics should fix on their side

Insurance confusion is not only a patient problem. It is also a provider operations problem.

When front-desk teams cannot confirm eligibility quickly, when clinicians order services without checking benefit rules, when pharmacy stock-outs are poorly documented, and when claims are not tracked cleanly, patients lose trust and hospitals lose money. That creates both revenue leakage and accountability problems.

For Nigerian hospitals trying to expand insured patient volume without creating front-desk chaos, that operational discipline matters as much as the insurance card itself.

## The bottom line

NHIA can reduce healthcare costs for many Nigerians, but it works best when patients understand three things clearly: how to enroll, which provider network they are using, and what the benefit package does not fully cover.

If you are a patient or family member, do not stop at "I have insurance." Ask which hospital is accredited, what your plan covers, and what your complaint route is if something goes wrong.

If you are a provider, the real test is not whether you accept NHIA. It is whether your facility can manage insurance workflows accurately enough to protect patients, reduce friction, and avoid silent revenue loss.
