High blood pressure is one of those problems that can quietly build for years. Many Nigerians only discover it after a headache, a stroke in the family, a failed pre-employment medical, or a routine BP check at a pharmacy or clinic.

That is why this is a timely topic. In May 2026, Nigeria's Federal Ministry of Health and Social Welfare said it had rolled out [policies and guidelines for early detection, management, and prevention of hypertension](https://health.gov.ng/hypertension-fg-rolled-out-policy-and-guidelines-for-early-detection-and-management/). Around the same time, a 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis reported that hypertension affects [about one in three Nigerian adults](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13088831/).

If you live in Lagos, Abuja, Port Harcourt, Kano, Enugu, Ibadan, or a smaller town where stress, traffic, salty convenience foods, poor sleep, and missed checkups are common, this matters.

## First: high blood pressure usually does not cause early symptoms

According to the [World Health Organization](https://www.who.int/en/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/hypertension), most people with hypertension do not feel any symptoms. That is why you should not wait until you "feel sick" before checking your blood pressure.

Very high blood pressure can cause warning signs such as:

- severe headache
- blurred vision
- chest pain
- shortness of breath
- weakness, numbness, or difficulty speaking

If a reading is **180/120 mm Hg or higher** and the person has any of those symptoms, treat it as urgent and get emergency medical help immediately.

## What blood pressure numbers usually mean

A blood pressure reading has two numbers:

- **Systolic**: the top number
- **Diastolic**: the bottom number

For most adults:

- **Below 120/80** is generally considered normal
- **120-129 with bottom number below 80** is higher than ideal
- **130-139 or 80-89** is a warning range that deserves follow-up
- **140/90 or higher**, especially on repeated checks, should be reviewed by a clinician

The [American Heart Association guide to blood pressure readings](https://www.heart.org/en/health-topics/high-blood-pressure/understanding-blood-pressure-readings) is useful for understanding common categories, but an actual diagnosis should come from a qualified health professional after proper repeat measurements.

WHO also notes that the best way to detect hypertension is to [measure blood pressure and assess risk with a health professional](https://www.who.int/en/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/hypertension).

## How to check your blood pressure properly

One bad reading after climbing stairs, arguing in traffic, rushing into the pharmacy, or drinking strong coffee does not tell the full story.

The [CDC guidance on self-measured blood pressure](https://www.cdc.gov/high-blood-pressure/measure/index.html) recommends a more careful approach:

- rest quietly before the reading
- do not smoke, exercise, or take caffeine shortly before checking
- sit with your back supported and feet flat on the floor
- keep your arm supported at chest height
- take at least two readings, 1 to 2 minutes apart
- write the numbers down with the date and time

In practical terms, if you buy a home BP monitor in Nigeria or use one at a pharmacy, do not panic over one number. Track a pattern.

## When should a Nigerian adult get checked?

You should check your blood pressure soon if:

- you are 30 or older and have not checked it recently
- a parent or sibling has hypertension, stroke, kidney disease, or diabetes
- you are overweight or gaining abdominal weight
- you often eat salty processed foods, instant noodles, seasoning-heavy meals, or fast food
- you drink alcohol heavily, smoke, or sleep poorly
- you are pregnant or recently delivered and have swelling, headaches, or visual symptoms

Even younger adults should not assume they are too young. Nigerian clinicians now see raised blood pressure in people in their 20s and 30s, especially where stress, inactivity, obesity, and family history overlap.

## What you can start doing this week

Lifestyle support matters, but it is not a substitute for diagnosis, testing, or prescribed treatment.

WHO recommends a [healthy diet](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/healthy-diet), less salt, physical activity, weight reduction where needed, and avoiding tobacco. WHO also recommends [less than 5 grams of salt per day](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/sodium-reduction) for adults, which is just under one teaspoon in total from all sources.

Practical steps that fit many Nigerian households:

- Taste food before adding extra salt.
- Cut down on instant noodles seasoning, processed meats, heavily salted snacks, and stock-cube-heavy cooking.
- Build more meals around beans, vegetables, fruits, fish, and less-fried options where possible.
- Aim for regular movement such as brisk walking, stair climbing, dancing, or market-road walking most days of the week.
- Reduce alcohol binges.
- Take prescribed medicines exactly as directed. Do not stop once you "feel fine."

For adults, WHO says [at least 150 minutes of moderate physical activity per week](https://www.who.int/initiatives/behealthy/physical-activity) is a useful target.

## What about garlic, zobo, supplements, or herbal mixtures?

Many families ask whether home remedies can "flush" high blood pressure. This is where people get into trouble.

Healthy eating, weight loss, exercise, and stress reduction can support blood pressure control. But **they are not emergency treatment**, and **herbs or supplements should not replace proper diagnosis or prescribed medicines**.

If you use herbal mixtures, bitters, supplements, or imported "BP support" products:

- tell your doctor or pharmacist exactly what you are taking
- avoid mixing them casually with prescription drugs
- do not assume "natural" means safe for the kidneys, liver, or pregnancy

If you want to try supportive diet changes, do that alongside proper clinic follow-up, not instead of it.

## Red flags: when to see a clinician urgently

Go to a hospital or urgent clinic quickly if:

- your blood pressure is **180/120 or higher**
- you have severe headache plus blurred vision
- you have chest pain or shortness of breath
- you develop facial droop, weakness, numbness, confusion, or trouble speaking
- you are pregnant and have headache, swelling, visual disturbance, or upper abdominal pain

Book a non-emergency clinic visit soon if:

- your home or pharmacy readings keep coming back **140/90 or higher**
- your readings are repeatedly borderline and you have diabetes, kidney disease, or prior stroke risk
- you were once on BP drugs and stopped them on your own

## For Nigerian clinics and hospitals, screening must be more consistent

This is not just a patient problem. It is a workflow problem too.

In many facilities, blood pressure is taken at triage but not tracked well over time. Follow-up appointments get missed. Repeat high readings are buried in paper notes. A patient can show up three times with elevated BP and still leave without a clear review plan.

## The bottom line

High blood pressure is common in Nigeria, often silent, and dangerous when ignored. The right next step is simple: **check it properly, repeat it, document it, and let a clinician interpret the pattern**.

If your number is high, do not rely on guesswork, prayer alone, roadside advice, or herbs alone. Get evaluated. Ask what tests you need. Ask whether you need treatment now or structured follow-up. That is how complications are prevented.
